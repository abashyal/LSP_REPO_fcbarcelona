# Question 4 – Reflection on AI in Programming

At the start of the semester, I thought of AI tools primarily as a shortcut for generating boilerplate code — something to save typing rather than something that genuinely aided understanding. Working through the assignments changed that view. When I used AI to help scaffold the IntegerSet implementation and the Strategy pattern refactoring, I found that the most useful part was not the generated code itself but the back-and-forth of checking whether the output was actually correct. Asking the AI to explain why it chose a particular design, and then verifying that reasoning against the lecture material, forced me to engage more deeply with the concepts than I might have otherwise. That said, I also noticed that AI tools can suggest plausible-looking but subtly wrong answers — especially on concurrency and design questions — which reinforced that I still need to understand the material well enough to catch mistakes.

Going forward, I plan to use AI as a first-draft and debugging partner rather than a final answer generator. For unfamiliar APIs or boilerplate setup I will lean on it heavily to reduce friction, but for design decisions, algorithmic correctness, and anything involving concurrency or security I will treat AI output as a hypothesis to be verified rather than a solution to be accepted. The habit of reading generated code critically and asking "why does this work?" is something I intend to carry into every future project.
